# Cely brand guide

This guide is the source of truth for how Cely looks, sounds, and behaves. It defines a new identity and does not draw from the styling of the current application.

## Brand at a glance

| Element | Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Name | Cely |
| Pronunciation | sell-ee |
| Brand essence | Understanding, carried safely into care |
| Tagline | Every concern, clearly carried |
| Extended line | Every concern, clearly carried into care |
| Positioning | Governed clinical workflow intelligence |
| Primary audience | Clinicians and leaders at independent and midsize outpatient practices |
| Patient expression | Warm, plainspoken, and conversational |
| Clinician expression | Concise, neutral, and evidence-first |

Cely is a product and a system, not a character. Do not give it a gender, personality biography, face, mascot, or assistant persona.

## Strategic foundation

### The problem Cely addresses

Important context can be lost between what a patient means, what the record contains, and what a clinician has time to review. This is especially risky when language, culture, uncertainty, or fragmented records make a concern harder to express.

### The promise

Cely helps a care team listen, clarify, confirm, connect, cite, review, and carry patient concerns into the visit. It preserves the patient's words, links relevant chart context, makes uncertainty visible, and keeps clinical authority with qualified people.

### What Cely is

- A governed way to turn patient concerns and chart context into an evidence-linked visit agenda.
- A workflow layer that records provenance, review, and approval.
- A system for turning approved workflows into reusable, testable skills.
- A shared experience for patients, clinicians, and practice teams.

### What Cely is not

- A diagnostic system.
- A replacement for a qualified medical interpreter.
- An autonomous clinical decision-maker.
- A mascot, virtual clinician, or human substitute.
- A promise of perfect translation, perfect understanding, or error-free care.

## Messaging hierarchy

Use the shortest level that gives the audience enough context. Do not stack every level in the same view.

### Primary line

> Every concern, clearly carried.

### Extended line

> Every concern, clearly carried into care.

### One-sentence description

> Cely turns patient concerns and chart context into an evidence-linked visit agenda, with patients and clinicians in control.

### Short product description

> Cely helps patients describe what matters in the language they prefer. It preserves their words, asks focused follow-up questions, and connects relevant chart evidence for clinician review. Approved work can become a governed skill that care teams can test and reuse.

### Practice-leader description

> Cely gives outpatient teams a consistent way to prepare for visits without hiding uncertainty or provenance. It connects the patient's account with relevant chart context, routes questions for review, and records clinician approval before work is reused.

### Claims boundary

Describe observable product behavior. Do not imply diagnosis, guaranteed outcomes, autonomous chart action, interpreter replacement, compliance certification, or zero data retention. Use qualified language for model behavior, such as “supports,” “suggests,” or “flags for review.”

Do not lead with model names, integrations, or technology categories. Mention them when they explain a concrete capability or implementation requirement.

## Voice principles

### Attentive

Lead with the patient's or clinician's need. Reflect the concern accurately before asking for more information.

- Use: “You said the pain starts in your toe. When did this episode begin?”
- Avoid: “Please provide additional symptom details.”

### Precise

Separate what was reported, what the chart shows, what Cely inferred, and what a clinician may choose to do.

- Use: “Patient report: pain began in the left great toe. Chart fact: allopurinol appears on the active medication list.”
- Avoid: “The patient has a gout flare and the medicine is failing.”

### Calm

State risk plainly and give the next action. Do not use alarmist language, celebration, or false reassurance.

- Use: “This answer may describe a time-sensitive symptom. Stop the intake and contact emergency services now.”
- Avoid: “Critical alert! We detected a dangerous emergency.”

### Culturally humble

Preserve the person's wording and ask what an unfamiliar expression means in context. Do not flatten cultural or spiritual language into a clinical conclusion.

- Use: “When you say ‘parang may gumagapang sa dibdib,’ do you mean a feeling on your skin or inside your chest?”
- Avoid: “The patient is describing paresthesia.”

### Accountable

Show the source and review state of important information. Name uncertainty instead of concealing it.

- Use: “Meaning not yet confirmed by the patient.”
- Avoid: “Translation complete.”

## Writing rules

### Use sentence case

Write headings, labels, navigation, buttons, status text, and metadata in sentence case. Preserve standard capitalization only for proper names and necessary acronyms such as Cely, Athena, and WCAG.

- Use: “Review visit agenda”
- Avoid: “REVIEW VISIT AGENDA”
- Avoid: “Review Visit Agenda”

Do not simulate hierarchy with uppercase lettering or wide letter spacing. Use size, weight, spacing, and placement instead.

### Write directly

- Put the person or action near the start of the sentence.
- Prefer concrete verbs: listen, clarify, confirm, connect, cite, review, and carry.
- Use one idea per sentence when the reader is under time pressure.
- Keep patient questions bounded. Ask for one answer at a time.
- Use active voice unless the actor is unknown or irrelevant.
- Use contractions in patient-facing copy when they sound natural.
- Use numerals for measurements, times, dates, and ordered agenda items.
- Use periods for complete sentences. Do not add periods to short button labels.

### Avoid model-shaped marketing language

Do not use formulaic contrasts such as “not just X, but Y” or broad constructions such as “whether you are X or Y.” Remove generic scene-setting about a changing industry. Do not describe the product with words such as “seamlessly,” “unlock,” “revolutionize,” “supercharge,” “elevate,” “delve,” or “game-changing.” Avoid “AI-powered” when the model is not the subject.

Also avoid:

- Repetitive three-part lists written for rhythm instead of meaning.
- Slogan fragments that do not communicate a fact.
- Repeated em dashes.
- Rhetorical questions used as headings.
- Manufactured comparisons with unnamed alternatives.
- Claims that the product is magical, effortless, autonomous, or universally intelligent.
- Friendly filler before an important action or safety instruction.

### Name people with respect

- Use “patients who prefer another language,” not deficit-framed labels.
- Use the language name a person gives for it.
- Use “patient” only in clinical contexts. Use “person” when clinical status is not relevant.
- Do not label a person as “noncompliant.” Describe the observable event and the reported reason.
- Do not infer identity, health literacy, immigration status, or cultural belief from language choice.

## Writing by context

### Patient intake

Explain why a question matters only when that context helps the patient answer. Ask one bounded question, then wait.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “Where is the pain strongest?” | “Tell me everything about the pain.” |
| “Did the dizziness start before or after your morning medicine?” | “Can you elaborate on your medication-related symptoms?” |
| “I understood that the pain began in your big toe. Is that right?” | “We have accurately translated your concern.” |

### Clinician handoff

Lead with the confirmed concern. Attach sources to individual facts. Keep patient report, chart fact, interpretation, and suggested next step visibly separate.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “Patient report: sharp left foot pain for 6 months, beginning at the great toe.” | “Chronic gout.” |
| “Chart fact: arthritis appears on the problem list, recorded 2025-11-04.” | “History confirms arthritis is the cause.” |
| “Interpretation: food-associated episodes may merit clarification. Patient has not confirmed a causal relationship.” | “Pork triggers the condition.” |
| “Suggested next step: review medication use and clarify the timing of each episode.” | “Change the medication.” |

### Safety escalation

State the reason, the action, and the limit of the product. Put the action first when delay may create harm.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “Call emergency services now. Sudden weakness on one side can be an emergency. Cely cannot assess this symptom.” | “You may want to consider seeking help.” |
| “Stop this intake and contact the on-call clinician. The patient reported a new symptom that matches the practice's urgent-review rule.” | “Urgent” shown only as a red badge. |

### Marketing

Connect a claim to a specific workflow and name the point of human control.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “Prepare an evidence-linked visit agenda before the appointment.” | “Transform care with intelligent automation.” |
| “Clinicians review the source and approve the next action.” | “Let Cely handle clinical work for you.” |
| “Preserve the patient's words while connecting relevant chart context.” | “Break every language barrier.” |

### Empty states

Say what is absent and what the person can do next.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “No patient concerns have been confirmed yet.” | “Nothing to see here.” |
| “No chart evidence matched this agenda item. Review the record or continue without a citation.” | “No data.” |

### Errors

Describe what happened, whether work was saved, and the safest next action. Do not blame the patient or clinician.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “The handoff was not sent. Your review is saved. Try again or copy the summary.” | “Something went wrong.” |
| “Athena did not accept this update. No chart data changed.” | “Integration failure: 422.” |

### Buttons and links

Start with a specific verb. Match the label to the result.

| Use | Avoid |
| --- | --- |
| “Confirm meaning” | “Yes” |
| “Review evidence” | “Learn more” |
| “Send for clinician review” | “Submit” |
| “Keep original wording” | “Cancel” |

### Culturally specific descriptions

Preserve the original phrase, provide a clearly labeled literal translation when useful, and ask a focused question. Never replace the phrase with a diagnosis.

> Original patient wording: “Parang may gumagapang sa dibdib ko.”
>
> Literal rendering: “It feels as though something is crawling in my chest.”
>
> Clarifying question: “Is that feeling on your skin or inside your chest?”

The original wording remains the primary source. A translation, interpretation, or summary must never overwrite it.

## Information and provenance

Use four distinct content roles in clinical views:

1. **Patient report:** The patient's confirmed wording or a clearly attributed summary.
2. **Chart fact:** A dated fact from the record, paired with its source.
3. **Interpretation:** A bounded inference with its confidence or unresolved question.
4. **Suggested next step:** An action offered for qualified human review.

Do not merge these roles into a single paragraph when doing so could make an inference look like a fact. Use labels, rules, source links, and spacing before adding containers.

Evidence identifiers may use IBM Plex Mono. The surrounding description should use IBM Plex Sans. A citation should identify its source and relevant date in accessible text, not only through an icon or color.

## Logo system

### Primary wordmark

The primary identity is a proper-case `Cely` wordmark. Its starting point is Newsreader Semibold, converted to custom vector outlines. The final drawing has:

- An open `C` with parallel-cut terminals.
- Unmodified, readable internal proportions through `e` and `l`.
- A `y` terminal that extends into a short horizontal carry line.
- Optical spacing designed for display between 80px and 320px wide.

The carry line represents meaning moving from a person's words to reviewed care. It is a structural part of the wordmark, not an underline or decorative flourish.

### Canonical assets

| File | Use |
| --- | --- |
| `/brand/cely-wordmark.svg` | Primary ink wordmark on canvas, paper, or other light backgrounds |
| `/brand/cely-wordmark-reversed.svg` | Paper-colored wordmark on ink or other approved dark backgrounds |
| `/brand/cely-wordmark-monochrome.svg` | Single-color reproduction when the primary palette is unavailable |

The favicon and app icon are constrained-format derivatives of the wordmark. They are not standalone symbols and should not replace the full wordmark when there is room for it.

### Clear space and minimum size

Keep clear space equal to the height of the lowercase `e` on every side of the wordmark. No text, rule, image edge, or interactive control may enter that area.

- Minimum wordmark width: 80px in digital use.
- Minimum favicon size: 16px.
- Prefer the full wordmark above 48px of available horizontal space.
- At small sizes, use the supplied derivative. Do not redraw or crop a new mark.

### Correct use

- Use the primary ink wordmark on canvas or paper.
- Use the reversed wordmark on ink.
- Keep the supplied aspect ratio and clear space.
- Use the SVG wherever the format supports it.
- Use accessible text naming Cely when the image is the only visible brand identifier.

### Incorrect use

- Do not recolor individual letters.
- Do not remove, lengthen, or animate the carry line.
- Do not stretch, skew, rotate, outline, or add a shadow.
- Do not place the wordmark inside a pill, badge, or decorative card.
- Do not add a heart, pulse line, medical cross, shield, robot, sparkle, face, or mascot.
- Do not typeset a substitute wordmark with a live font.
- Do not use the favicon derivative as a recurring decorative icon.

## Typography

### Families

- **Display:** [Newsreader](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Newsreader), used for the wordmark source, major headlines, and sparse editorial emphasis.
- **Interface and body:** [IBM Plex Sans](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/IBM+Plex+Sans), used for controls, content, forms, and navigation.
- **Evidence and technical metadata:** IBM Plex Mono, used for short source identifiers, timestamps when alignment matters, and code.
- **Multilingual fallback:** The matching script-specific IBM Plex family where available, then [Noto](https://fonts.google.com/noto), then the platform sans-serif fallback.

Use the optical size or text cut appropriate to the rendered size when the font source supports it. Never synthesize bold or italic styles that the loaded family does not provide.

### Type scale

| Role | Size / line height | Typical weight | Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Display | 56px / 60px | 600 | One short statement on large canvases |
| Heading 1 | 40px / 44px | 600 | Page title |
| Heading 2 | 28px / 34px | 600 | Major section |
| Body | 16px / 24px | 400 | Default reading text and form content |
| Supporting | 14px / 20px | 400 or 500 | Sources, helper text, and secondary actions |
| Label | 12px / 16px | 500 | Short metadata and form labels |

Do not use the 12px label style for long content or primary actions. Avoid body text below 16px in patient-facing views. Use weight and spacing with restraint; do not replace hierarchy with all caps.

## Layout and composition

### Editorial structure

- Use a 4px base spacing unit and the tokenized scale.
- Keep long-form reading columns near 720px.
- Use a 12-column grid for wide product views and collapse predictably at narrow widths.
- Align related content to a shared edge.
- Separate sections with whitespace or a thin rule before adding a background surface.
- Use 4px, 8px, or 12px corner radii according to the size of the bounded object.
- Use minimal elevation. A border or change in surface color is usually enough.

### Cards

A card is appropriate only when its boundary communicates ownership, interaction, or selection. Valid examples include a discrete patient record, a confirmation requiring a response, and a review task that can move independently.

Do not:

- Put every paragraph, metric, agenda item, or navigation option in a card.
- Nest cards.
- Float a panel only to make a page look more designed.
- Repeat the page background with a bordered rectangle that has no semantic boundary.
- Use card grids as a default layout for prose.

### Badges and pills

Use a badge only for a compact, actionable state such as “Urgent,” “Needs review,” “Confirmed,” or “Unavailable.” Pair it with visible text or an instruction that works without color.

Do not use badges for integrations, model names, technology labels, decorative metadata, feature promotion, or general categorization. Do not turn ordinary buttons, filters, navigation, or labels into pills. A pill shape is acceptable only when it communicates a known control behavior, such as a segmented selection or removable filter.

### Surfaces and effects

- Do not use gradients or glass effects.
- Avoid oversized corner radii and excessive shadows.
- Do not make chat bubbles the dominant page structure.
- Avoid decorative dashboard chrome.
- Use a maximum of one subtle elevation level in standard content and reserve overlay elevation for menus, dialogs, and popovers.

## Carry-line motif

The carry line is a thin iris rule that connects three stages:

1. The patient's wording.
2. Supporting evidence or clarification.
3. The reviewed action.

Use it to explain provenance or sequence, not to decorate empty space. Each connected point must remain understandable without the line. In reduced-motion mode, show the complete line immediately. Never animate the wordmark's carry line.

## Motion

- Interface transitions: 160ms.
- Explanatory transitions: 240ms.
- Easing: `cubic-bezier(0.2, 0.8, 0.2, 1)`.
- Animate opacity and transform when movement adds continuity.
- Avoid bounce, overshoot, celebration, looping decoration, and large parallax effects.
- Do not delay safety instructions, validation errors, or primary content for animation.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`; remove nonessential motion and complete state changes immediately.

## Accessibility

- Meet WCAG 2.2 AA for text, controls, focus indicators, and interaction states.
- Keep normal text contrast at or above 4.5:1 and large text at or above 3:1.
- Use the documented foreground and background pairs. Do not assume a semantic color works on a different surface. Verified sRGB contrast ratios include ink on canvas at 16.68:1, muted ink on canvas at 9.18:1, iris on canvas at 7.63:1, ink on paper at 15.31:1, and muted ink on paper at 8.43:1.
- Provide a visible 3px iris focus outline with a 2px offset.
- Keep interactive targets at least 44px by 44px where space permits.
- Do not encode meaning through color, a badge, position, motion, or icon alone.
- Keep zoom and text resizing usable to 200 percent without loss of content or action.
- Write useful names for controls. Decorative assets should have empty alternative text.
- Preserve a logical heading order and reading order across responsive layouts.
- Announce urgent changes and errors to assistive technology without moving focus unexpectedly.

## Multilingual behavior

### Preserve source language

Store and display the patient's original words unchanged. A translation or clinician summary is a separate derived object with a language label, source, and review state.

When meaning is uncertain:

1. Preserve the phrase.
2. Say what is unclear.
3. Ask one focused question in the language the patient is using.
4. Carry both the original wording and the confirmed meaning into clinician review.

Do not silently normalize culturally specific expressions or replace them with clinical terminology.

### Layout and direction

- Use script-aware font fallback rather than forcing Latin glyph metrics.
- Set language metadata on each passage and use `dir="auto"` for user-entered content.
- For Arabic and other right-to-left scripts, mirror reading order and directional controls, but do not mirror the Cely wordmark, numerals, medication names, or standard media controls.
- Isolate mixed-direction dates, identifiers, and medication names so punctuation remains readable.
- Allow text to wrap. Do not truncate a patient statement or safety instruction to preserve layout.
- Budget for text expansion of at least 30 percent in controls and 50 percent in explanatory content.

### Test strings

Use these synthetic prompts when reviewing font fallback, line height, alignment, and direction:

- Tagalog: “Saan pinakamasakit?”
- Spanish: “¿Dónde siente el dolor con más intensidad?”
- Arabic: “أين تشعر بالألم أكثر؟”
- Hindi: “दर्द सबसे ज़्यादा कहाँ है?”
- Japanese: “痛みがいちばん強いのはどこですか。”

Test translated and untranslated passages together. Verify that diacritics are not clipped, Arabic remains connected, Devanagari marks render at the correct height, and CJK punctuation follows the selected locale.

## Asset and implementation rules

- Use the values in `cely.tokens.css` as the stable interface for future product styling.
- Keep wordmark SVGs as outlined paths with clean viewboxes and no live-font dependency.
- Do not inline alternate colors into the primary wordmark asset. Use the supplied variants.
- Keep the brand kit independent of application styles until a separate redesign phase is approved.
- Do not copy visual patterns from the current application into new brand assets.
- Use only synthetic people, records, and clinical examples in public brand material.

## Review checklist

Before publishing a Cely experience or asset, confirm that:

- The name, pronunciation, tagline, and positioning match this guide.
- Text uses sentence case and direct language.
- No model-shaped filler, inflated claim, or unsupported outcome claim remains.
- Patient report, chart fact, interpretation, and suggested next step are distinct.
- Original-language wording is preserved and attributed.
- Safety instructions name both the reason and the next action.
- Cards are limited to genuinely bounded objects.
- Badges communicate actionable state and are not decorative.
- Status remains understandable without color, shape, or icon alone.
- The wordmark has the required clear space and is at least 80px wide.
- Color contrast, focus, keyboard use, zoom, text expansion, and reduced motion have been tested.
- Every person, record, and clinical scenario shown is synthetic.
